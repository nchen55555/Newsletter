Published: 11/18/2025

Founders never stop searching for top talent, and that’s exactly how Phoebe Gates, Phia’s co-founder and daughter of Bill Gates, and I met: at a talent event shortly after Phia closed its $8M seed round led by Kleiner Perkins. What immediately stood out about Phoebe was how articulate and clear she was in communicating Phia’s long-term vision. She outlined the company’s trajectory with impressive precision and, most notably, was unafraid to push back during our discussion about Phia’s partnership with The Niche, demonstrating the kind of principled stubbornness and conviction often found in confident founders.

Phoebe and her co-founder, Sophia Kianni, met at Stanford, where they began laying the foundation for Phia, a fashion-tech startup designed to help shoppers make smarter, more sustainable purchasing decisions. Phia’s app and browser extension aggregate hundreds of millions of product listings, comparing prices across retailers, evaluating resale value, and surfacing second-hand alternatives. By integrating AI to answer questions like “Should I buy this?” or “Is there a better deal?”, the platform tackles a familiar pain point: users juggling countless tabs in search of the best price, especially in markets like New York, California, and Texas.

The Classic Chicken and Egg Problem

In writing this company profile, I was especially interested in how Phoebe and Sophia approached the classic chicken-and-egg problem underlying their business model: retailers only want to partner with platforms that already have committed users, while users only join if reliable inventory is available. Phia broke this cycle by prioritizing brand partnerships first, securing both direct and indirect retailers even before full monetization was possible. Their revenue model is built primarily on affiliate fees: Phia earns a flat kickback whenever a user clicks through and purchases from one of its 5,000 direct brand partners, supplemented by optional paid “spotlight” placements on the homepage. Other indirect partners contribute to GMV without yet generating revenue. Because full monetization wasn’t possible early on, onboarding brands upfront was essential. In parallel, Phia rapidly grew its user base through high-visibility channels, most notably a podcast featuring celebrity guests.
